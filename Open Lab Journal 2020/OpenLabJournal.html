I just had a great idea for a micrometeorite catcher device! I envision something with a large dish, like a radio dish. The lowest point of the dish contains a drain to a set of pipes and magnets. The pipes are to be arranged so that rainwater flows past magnets at different speeds, starting with high speed and slowing down to low speed. This would essentially make a magnetic field/mass spectrometer, passively run with rainwater!!!

The rim of the dish could be fit with a drip line, so that the dish could be washed out with measured amounts of water in dry climates. Ideally plastic would be used to make the dish to prevent metallic contaminants.

To collect samples, a dish can be set at the bottom of the line. Remove the bottom magnet to let the smallest sediment fall back into suspension, and flush the line with distilled water. Set aside the collection dish, and use another for the next magnet, and so on. The top magnet will collect only the largest particles, while the bottom will collect the smallest. Since it is a passive collector, it could be set in a field with a lightning rod and left for months. This would be an excellent upgrade to any large radio telescope, since the resolution would not be affected by a small drain directly under the antena.

Thoughts on Controlling a Thruster Suit

I have for a while thought about the best way to control an ‘Iron Man Suit’ or a thruster suit. The technology is still pretty far away, and in a world built by people without a thruster suit there is little application for them. That said, they still seem to enamor people around the world. One thing that seems to escape every convincing build I have seen so far is a decent control system. They all seem to rely on some kind of switches or toggles, usually controlled like a joystick or palmswitch. This is low tech and works in a limited capacity, but falls short when it comes to effectiveness. With low tech solutions, some kind of capability is sacrificed to operate the gear, like holding objects, vision, even more.

My inspiration comes from video games where you do not necessarily control the walking movements themselves, only the direction and duration of motion. This reduces an action that would ordinarily require hundreds of muscle groups into a single thumb motion. When the player moves, the fundamental quality of motion is decided by the player; things like footstep positions, arm swing, and even head bob are decided and conveyed by the computer. Players can dedicate concentration into precise tasks like aim and pickups without needing to worry about fumbling with where their hands are.

While this sounds good, it is impossible to comfortably implement. When watching players interact with games, they make involuntary motions outside the game that correspond to in game motions. The best illustration of this is watching a young child play a racing game with cars, where the child will lean left and right in their seat as they attempt to steer the vehicle. If a control interface were to neglect these natural motions, it would be uncomfortable and unnatural feeling for a user. At the worst, it could lead to dangerous interactions between the user and the suit, like thruster-fuselage contact.

Finally, a lesson in biomechanics straight from bulldogger Bill Pickett: where the head goes, the body follows. Whether tackling people or steers, all mammals can easily be lead by the head. Martial artists also follow the rule, using the position of an opponents head over their center of mass to determine incoming attacks. Control systems for flight interface should make use of this general rule in biomechanics.

An ideal flight suit interface would have these general qualities, listed simply below:

  1. Make big motions simple.
  2. Require no sacrifices from other motions and actions.
  3. Body motion leads flight suit motion, not the other way around.
  4. Natural movement should be allowed by the suit without compromising safety.
  5. Motion direction can be decided by head position without significantly limiting the operator.

The best interface one could use for this would be a neural response of some type, so that thrusters are controlled directly by the brain. This is a very complex method, but would be sufficient for multi-thrust vector systems. The second best is a system with natural motions turned into flight motions. An excellent starting point would be a compilation of natural motions by jumping, falling, skydiving, and driving as they correspond to movements in real space.

Falling

When humans fall, they will flail their arms about in a classic ‘windmill’ pattern. This is an involuntary response, and can be seen in people who fall from great hights. As such, any flight suit should be able to automatically recognise this involuntary response and kick in emergency stabilization to return to a normal orientation and mitigate the fall. This would require sensor feedback from the suit so that the controller would be able to determine orientation of the body as well as airspeed and velocity. If the user is windmilling, thrusters should fire to reorient the body.

When people fall in a controlled manner, like skydivers, they will assume postures that correspond with the desired motion. For moving in a direction, they tuck their body into an arrow and use the lift to move in their desired direction. At the same time, they will point their head up and in the direction of travel, since they want to see where they are going. The flight suit could check for these conditions and activate thrust in the direction that the user is looking.

To slow their fall or maintain position within the air column, skydivers will splay their limbs and assume a ‘neutral stance’ where the drag forces are balanced across the body. At the same time, they typically do not look up unless interacting with other skydivers. Thrusters would ideally perform a stabilizing routine for this set of motions, so that the diver does not tumble, or would turn off and orient for air cooling to prevent contact with nearby skydivers. If the operator were to spread all limbs like a starfish to increase drag and slow down, the thrusters should fire to reduce speed and reorient to the vertical. This would enable a propulsive landing from freefall.

Finally, the flight computer should be able to calculate the distance to terra firma at all times, and the permissible suicide burn. Operators should NEVER be able to go past the suicide burn floor, or serious injury will result. In event that the user passes the suicide burn floor, the suit should automatically assume a suicide burn. If the suit cannot make a suicide burn, it should assume a paratrooper landing roll to the side. This will mitigate injury to the pilot.

Driving

When driving, people make involuntary side to side motions and leans that counter the real or imaginary g forces experienced. Further, when seated in office chairs people will tilt forward and backwards when moving in those directions. This can be further observed in horseback riding, walking, and balancing. If the user makes a lean, it is to counter an expected force and should correspond to a force or rotation of equal magnitude. This would allow a user to spin about without dropping things or interrupting their natural motion. It would feel natural to the user, and would allow thrusters on the lean side to throttle down, preventing burns.

There should be no instance where the user leans, and the suit spins the other direction, as this will compromise stability for the operator.

/*

 * Thermistor Temperature Sensor Displayed on 4 Digit 7 segment display

 * Created by Liam Plybon, blablabliam.github.io

 *

 * Temperature is read from a thermistor to an Arduino Uno, calculated with general

 * data for a 10k NTC Thermistor, and transmitted bitwise to a shift register and

 * 7 segment display with four digits. Could be done with a nano as well, but this

 * was easier for prototyping.

 */

/*

 * temp control to get temp in K. Seems off for my thermistor by quite a bit,

 * around 5 degrees at STP and 6 at 0 C.

 */

int ThermistorPin = 0;

int Vo;

float R1 = 10000;

float logR2, R2, T;

float c1 = 1.009249522e-03, c2 = 2.378405444e-04, c3 = 2.019202697e-07;

/*

 * Display control

 * This determines how digits will be displayed bitwise, as well as the pins for the

 * shift registor and digit pins.

 */

const int digitPins[4] = {

  4,5,6,7};                 //4 common anode pins of the display

const int clockPin = 11;    //74HC595 Pin 11

const int latchPin = 12;    //74HC595 Pin 12

const int dataPin = 13;     //74HC595 Pin 14

const byte digit[10] =      //seven segment digits in bits

{

  B11000000, //0 B00111111

  B11111001, //1 B00000110

  B10100100, //2 B10100100

  B10110000, //3 B10110000

  B10011001, //4 B10011001

  B10010010, //5 B10010010

  B10000010, //6 B10000010

  B11111000, //7 B11111000

  B10000000, //8 B10000000

  B10010000  //9 B10010000

};

int digitBuffer[4] = {

  0};

int digitScan = 0, flag=0,  soft_scaler = 0;

;

/*

 * Setup pins

 */

void setup(){                

  for(int i=0;i<4;i++)

  {

    pinMode(digitPins[i],OUTPUT);

  }

  pinMode(latchPin, OUTPUT);

  pinMode(clockPin, OUTPUT);

  pinMode(dataPin, OUTPUT);  

}

 

//writes the temperature to display

void updateDisp(){

  //Set all digits to off

  for(byte j=0; j<4; j++)  

    digitalWrite(digitPins[j], HIGH);

  //send clear to shift register

  digitalWrite(latchPin, LOW);  

  shiftOut(dataPin, clockPin, MSBFIRST, B00000000);

  digitalWrite(latchPin, HIGH);

  //wait for digit to clear

  delayMicroseconds(100);

  digitalWrite(digitPins[digitScan], LOW);

 

  digitalWrite(latchPin, LOW);  

  if(digitScan==2)

    shiftOut(dataPin, clockPin, MSBFIRST, ~(digit[digitBuffer[digitScan]] | B10000000)); //print the decimal point on the 3rd digit

  else

    shiftOut(dataPin, clockPin, MSBFIRST, ~digit[digitBuffer[digitScan]]);

 

  digitalWrite(latchPin, HIGH);

  digitScan++;

  if(digitScan>3) digitScan=0;

}

 

void loop(){

  //calculates the temp in K, then writes the temp to the display  

  Vo = analogRead(ThermistorPin);

  R2 = R1 * (1023.0 / (float)Vo - 1.0);

  logR2 = log(R2);

  T = (1.0 / (c1 + c2*logR2 + c3*logR2*logR2*logR2));

  //Convert to Celcius

  T = T - 273.15;

  //Farenheit conversion

  //T = (T * 9.0)/ 5.0 + 32.0;  

  digitBuffer[3] = int(T)/1000;

  digitBuffer[2] = (int(T)%1000)/100;

  digitBuffer[1] = (int(T)%100)/10;

  digitBuffer[0] = (int(T)%100)%10;

  updateDisp();

  delay(2);

}

The code works well enough for Github, I think. Maybe I should just throw a board together and convert the pins to a nano? Would be easy.
